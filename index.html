Day 1 — Kobe Airport → Dotonbori

Things to see:
  • Meriken Park — Waterfront park built on reclaimed port land and opened in 1987 as part of Kobe’s modern harbor redevelopment. The area preserves Kobe’s identity as one of Japan’s major international ports after its 1868 opening to foreign trade.
  • Kobe Port Tower — Kobe’s red hyperboloid observation tower opened in 1963 and became one of the city’s strongest skyline symbols. Its form was designed to evoke a Japanese tsuzumi hand drum.
  • BE KOBE Monument — A post-earthquake civic monument introduced after the 1995 Great Hanshin-Awaji Earthquake recovery era. It represents Kobe’s rebuilt waterfront identity and is now one of the harbor’s signature photo landmarks.
  • Harborland — Large shopping and entertainment district developed on the west side of Kobe’s port in the 1990s. It ties the historic harbor edge to the modern city waterfront.
  • Dotonbori River Walk — The canal district grew around entertainment theaters during the Edo period and became Osaka’s best-known nightlife corridor. Its neon, food signs, and bridge views define the modern Dotonbori image.
  • Glico Sign — The running-man sign has advertised Ezaki Glico above Dotonbori since 1935, with the current version continuing one of Osaka’s most recognizable commercial landmarks. It is closely associated with Ebisubashi Bridge and the city’s food-and-neon culture.

Day 3 — Dotonbori → Uji / Southern Kyoto

Things to see:
  • Ōte-mon Gate — Main western gate of Osaka Castle’s outer defenses, rebuilt during the Tokugawa reconstruction of the castle in the 1620s. It shows the scale and formality of the castle’s official approach.
  • Nishinomaru Garden — Former western bailey area of Osaka Castle, now a paid garden known for wide lawns and cherry blossoms. It gives one of the clearest exterior views of the main tower across the moat and stone walls.
  • Osaka Castle — Toyotomi Hideyoshi began the original castle in 1583 as a symbol of national unification power. The current main tower dates to 1931, but the site remains tied to the Toyotomi regime and the Siege of Osaka in 1614–1615.
  • Hokoku Shrine — Shrine honoring Toyotomi Hideyoshi, the warlord most strongly associated with Osaka Castle’s rise. Its presence inside the park keeps the castle’s Toyotomi legacy visible beyond the main tower.
  • Osaka Castle Park Fountain — East-side park landmark within the broad public grounds surrounding Osaka Castle. It marks the transition from the central castle zone into the modern park and event areas.
  • Cool Japan Park Osaka WW Hall — Modern theater complex opened in 2019 as part of Osaka’s entertainment and cultural-event infrastructure. Its placement beside the castle park shows how the historic grounds now function as both heritage space and city venue.
  • Forest of Memorial Trees — Quiet wooded section of Osaka Castle Park with a more reflective atmosphere than the main tourist path. It adds a softer memorial landscape between the theater area and open plazas.
  • Sun Square — Open plaza area on the east side of Osaka Castle Park used as a circulation and gathering space. It reflects the park’s modern role as a public recreation zone around the historic castle core.
  • Baseball field — Athletic field within Osaka Castle Park, showing how the former military and castle grounds were converted into public urban parkland. It is a reminder that the area functions as a living city park, not only a preserved monument.
  • Artillery Arsenal area — Osaka Army Arsenal operated in this wider area from the Meiji period until World War II and was one of Japan’s major military production sites. Its remnants and memory add a modern wartime layer to the older castle landscape.
  • Plum Grove — Seasonal grove inside Osaka Castle Park known for late-winter and early-spring plum blossoms. It adds a classical garden element to the fortress landscape before the cherry blossom season peaks.
  • Aoyamon Gate — Historic gate on the castle’s northeast side near the inner moat and Plum Grove. It helps mark the transition from garden/park space back toward the fortified castle core.
  • Gokuraku-bashi Bridge — Bridge crossing the inner moat toward the north side of Osaka Castle’s central enclosure. It provides one of the strongest moat-and-stone-wall perspectives near the main tower.
  • Site of Haunted House — Small curiosity marker near the edge of the castle-area route. It is not a major historic site, but it adds a strange local-map detail to the end of the castle walk.
  • Higo Stone — One of the named giant stones in Osaka Castle’s fortifications, part of the massive Tokugawa-era stonework that rebuilt the castle after the Toyotomi period. These stones show the engineering and daimyo labor power used to remake the fortress.
  • FamilyMart Tanimachi — Modern Japanese convenience-store chain founded in 1973 and now common across urban Japan. Here it serves as a clear neighborhood landmark between Osaka Castle and Tanimachi 4-chome Station.
  • Tokon-do Temple — Eastern Golden Hall of Kōfuku-ji, originally established in 726 during the Nara period. The hall links the walk to the powerful Fujiwara clan temple complex that shaped early Nara Buddhism.
  • Kasuga Pagoda Ruins — Remains tied to the larger Kasuga/Kōfuku-ji sacred landscape on the edge of Nara Park. The ruins hint at how dense the old temple-shrine zone once was around the Fujiwara institutions.
  • Nara National Museum — National museum opened in 1895 and best known for Buddhist art, sculpture, and ritual objects. Its location beside Nara Park places it directly inside the old capital’s temple-and-shrine corridor.
  • Meiji Emperor’s Throne Site — Historical marker connected to imperial presence in Nara during the modern Meiji period. It adds a nineteenth-century imperial layer to an area better known for Nara-period Buddhism.
  • Deer Park — Central deer area of Nara Park, where the deer are traditionally regarded as messengers of the Kasuga deity. Nara Park was formally established in 1880, but the sacred-deer tradition is much older.
  • Sacred Deer Statue — Modern marker honoring the deer’s religious and cultural role in Nara. It reflects the connection between the park’s famous deer and Kasuga Taisha’s shrine tradition.
  • Second Torii Gate — Gate on the approach into the Kasuga Taisha side of Nara Park. It signals the shift from open deer park into the shrine’s more sacred wooded approach.
  • Honsha Osugi / 本社大杉 — Great cedar associated with the Kasuga Taisha main precinct. Large old trees are part of the shrine’s sacred forest atmosphere and help define the approach to the main shrine.
  • Kasuga Taisha main precinct area — Kasuga Taisha was founded in 768 as the tutelary shrine of the Fujiwara clan. The shrine is famous for its lanterns, sacred forest, and role in Nara’s UNESCO-listed historic landscape.
  • Mizuya Shrine — Small shrine within the Kasuga Taisha sacred precinct. It adds one of the many subsidiary-shrine layers that make the approach feel dense and ritual-focused.
  • Sansha Takusen Pond — Pond waypoint within the shrine-to-Tōdai-ji walking zone. It provides a quieter water feature between the Kasuga side and the large Tōdai-ji gate area.
  • Nandaimon Gate — Great South Gate of Tōdai-ji, rebuilt in 1199 after war damage. It is famous for its huge guardian statues by the Kei school sculptors Unkei and Kaikei.
  • Nekodan Cat Stairway — Small named stairway on the east-side Tōdai-ji approach. It is a minor local route detail rather than a primary temple monument.
  • Todai-ji Nagaike — Pond near the Tōdai-ji side of the park route. It creates a calm transition before the final temple-area endpoint.
  • Great Ginkgo Tree — Large ginkgo landmark near the Tōdai-ji route endpoint. Ginkgo trees are common around temples and shrines and often become seasonal yellow-leaf markers in autumn.
  • Todaiji Temple Kaidan Hall — Ordination-hall area connected to Tōdai-ji’s role as one of ancient Japan’s most important Buddhist institutions. Tōdai-ji was founded in the eighth century and became the head temple of state Buddhism in Nara.

Nintendo, Uji River, and Torii Run

Day 4 — Okubo → Uji → Fushimi Inari → Sanjo

Things to see:
  • Statue of Murasaki Shikibu — Statue honoring Murasaki Shikibu, the Heian-period author of The Tale of Genji. Uji is strongly tied to the novel’s final “Uji Chapters,” making this a literary starting point for the river route.
  • Byodo-in Omotesando / Uji Tea Street — Traditional approach street leading toward Byodo-in, lined with Uji tea shops and sweets. Uji tea has been famous since the medieval period and became one of Kyoto’s signature regional products.
  • Byodo-in Front Gate / South Gate — Byodo-in was founded in 1052 and is known for Phoenix Hall, completed in 1053. The gate pair defines the planned entry and exit through the paid temple grounds.
  • Uji River island crossing — Scenic bridge-and-island crossing through the middle of the Uji River. It gives the day a stronger river route than simply crossing Uji Bridge.
  • Uji Shrine / Ujigami Shrine — Uji Shrine is tied to Prince Uji no Wakiiratsuko, while nearby Ujigami Shrine preserves some of Japan’s oldest shrine architecture. Together they anchor the east-bank sacred area.
  • Daikichiyama Observation Deck — Hilltop viewpoint above Uji that overlooks the river, Byodo-in area, and central Uji. The climb adds a final elevated view to the Uji River section before leaving for the train.
  • Fushimi Inari lower shrine route — Compact lower-route version of Kyoto’s major Inari shrine, focused on the main approach, Senbon Torii, and ritual stops. It avoids the full mountain hike while still covering the iconic shrine core.
  • Senbon Torii — Dense corridor of donated vermilion torii gates and the shrine’s most famous visual feature. The gates are offerings from individuals and businesses, turning the mountain path into a layered record of devotion and sponsorship.
  • Sanjo / Shimomaruyacho area — Central Kyoto stay area close to Sanjo, Kawaramachi, Kiyamachi, and the next day’s east-Kyoto walking setup.
